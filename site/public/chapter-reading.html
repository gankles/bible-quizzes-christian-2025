Genesis 1

31 verses

1

In the beginning God created the heaven and the earth.

Commentary: This opening verse establishes the fundamental truth of creation. The Hebrew word "bara" (created) emphasizes God's unique creative power. The phrase "heaven and earth" encompasses all of creation—the spiritual and physical realms.

2

And the earth was without form, and void; and darkness was upon the face of the deep. And the Spirit of God moved upon the face of the waters.

Commentary: The earth's initial state is described as formless and empty. The presence of God's Spirit moving over the waters foreshadows the creative work to follow. This verse emphasizes God's active involvement in creation from the very beginning.

3

And God said, Let there be light: and there was light.

Commentary: Light is the first creation, symbolizing God's power and the dispelling of darkness. The phrase "Let there be" demonstrates God's creative authority—His word alone brings things into existence. Light is often associated with truth, goodness, and God's presence throughout Scripture.

4

And God saw the light, that it was good: and God divided the light from the darkness.

Commentary: God's evaluation of His creation as "good" appears throughout Genesis 1. This establishes that creation is inherently good and reflects God's character. The division of light and darkness represents order and distinction in creation.

5

And God called the light Day, and the darkness he called Night. And the evening and the morning were the first day.

Commentary: The naming of light and darkness establishes the first day. The Hebrew understanding of a day runs from evening to morning, reflecting the pattern of rest followed by work. This structure sets the pattern for the six days of creation.

6

And God said, Let there be a firmament in the midst of the waters: and let it divide the waters from the waters.

Commentary: The "firmament" (Hebrew "raqia") refers to the sky or expanse. God creates a separation between waters above and below, establishing the atmosphere. This demonstrates God's orderly arrangement of creation.

7

And God made the firmament, and divided the waters which were under the firmament from the waters which were above the firmament: and it was so.

Commentary: The execution of God's command is immediate and complete. The phrase "and it was so" emphasizes the certainty and power of God's word. What He speaks comes to pass without delay or difficulty.

8

And God called the firmament Heaven. And the evening and the morning were the second day.

Commentary: The sky is named "Heaven," establishing the spatial structure of creation. The completion of the second day follows the same pattern as the first, with evening and morning marking the day's boundaries.

9

And God said, Let the waters under the heaven be gathered together unto one place, and let the dry land appear: and it was so.

Commentary: God commands the waters to gather, creating dry land. This separation of water and land is essential for life. The obedience of creation to God's word demonstrates the harmony of the created order.

10

And God called the dry land Earth; and the gathering together of the waters called he Seas: and God saw that it was good.

Commentary: God names the dry land "Earth" and the waters "Seas," establishing the basic geography of our world. The repeated affirmation that creation is "good" reinforces that God's work is perfect and purposeful. This completes the third day of creation.
